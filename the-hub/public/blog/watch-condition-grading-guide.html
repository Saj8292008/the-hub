The Complete Guide to Watch Condition Grading

Master the grading standards and never overpay for condition again

Updated: February 2026 • 9 min read

Condition is everything in the pre-owned watch market. The difference between "Mint" and "Excellent" can be thousands of dollars on a Rolex Submariner. Yet most buyers don't understand what these grades actually mean. This guide decodes the watch grading scale used by dealers, collectors, and auction houses worldwide.

Why Condition Grading Matters

Unlike cars with standardized Carfax reports, watches have no universal grading system. Dealers use subjective terminology, leading to confusion and inflated prices. Understanding these standards helps you:

  • Spot overpriced listings - Don't pay "Mint" prices for "Excellent" condition
  • Negotiate effectively - Use specific condition issues to justify lower offers
  • Set realistic expectations - Know what condition to expect at each price point
  • Identify value opportunities - "Good" condition can be 40% cheaper with minor cosmetic issues

The Standard Watch Grading Scale

The industry uses six primary condition grades, from best to worst. Here's what each actually means:

1. New Old Stock (NOS) / Unworn

Definition: Brand new, never worn, factory sealed or with all original stickers intact.

Price premium: 5-15% above typical pre-owned

What to expect:

  • Plastic protective stickers on case and bracelet (if applicable)
  • Factory hang tags and protective sleeves present
  • Absolutely zero signs of wear anywhere
  • Original factory shine on all polished surfaces
  • Bracelet stickers intact showing no sizing adjustments
  • Complete full set: box, papers, warranty card (unstamped or recent date)

Red flags: If a dealer claims "NOS" but stickers are removed, warranty dated years ago, or any scratches exist—walk away. True NOS watches are rare and command premiums for good reason.

2. Mint / Like New In Box (LNIB)

Definition: Worn once or twice, essentially indistinguishable from new to the naked eye.

Price premium: Same as typical pre-owned market rate

What to expect:

  • Potentially very light desk diving marks (hairline scratches only visible under magnification)
  • No visible scratches, dings, or blemishes to naked eye
  • All polished surfaces maintain factory finish
  • Bracelet shows no stretch, clasp perfect
  • Crystal flawless with no scratches or chips
  • Minimal to no signs of daily wear
  • Complete set with box and papers standard (but not required for "Mint" grade)

Reality check: "Mint" is the most abused term in watch sales. Many dealers call anything without major damage "Mint." True mint condition means you can't tell it was worn without a loupe. Be skeptical and demand detailed photos.

3. Excellent / Very Good

Definition: Light wear consistent with careful ownership, no significant damage.

Price discount: 5-10% below mint/LNIB

What to expect:

  • Minor hairline scratches on case and bracelet
  • Light desk diving marks on clasp and case back
  • Brushed surfaces may show light wear patterns
  • Polished surfaces might have very fine swirls (removable with polishing)
  • Crystal remains scratch-free
  • No dents, deep scratches, or structural damage
  • Watch looks great from normal viewing distance

Sweet spot: This is the best value grade. "Excellent" watches look fantastic on the wrist but cost significantly less than "Mint" simply due to minor cosmetic wear most people never notice. Learn more about finding value in this condition tier.

4. Good / Average

Definition: Visible wear throughout but mechanically sound and structurally intact.

Price discount: 15-25% below mint

What to expect:

  • Noticeable scratches on case, bezel, and bracelet
  • Polished surfaces show visible wear and scratching
  • Possible small dings on case edges or lugs
  • Bracelet may show stretch or wear
  • Crystal might have light scratches (but no chips/cracks)
  • Overall appearance is "well-loved" but respectable
  • No damage affecting function or water resistance

Budget buyer's friend: "Good" condition is perfect for daily wearers who plan to add their own patina anyway. A professional polish can elevate "Good" to "Excellent" appearance for $100-200, making this grade a value play.

5. Fair / Below Average

Definition: Heavy wear with possible damage, may need service or repair.

Price discount: 30-50% below mint

What to expect:

  • Heavy scratching throughout case and bracelet
  • Visible dents, dings, or deep gouges
  • Polished areas may be heavily worn or dulled
  • Crystal likely scratched, possibly chipped
  • Bracelet stretched or damaged
  • Possible mechanical issues (service history unknown)
  • Missing bracelet links or components common

Proceed with caution: "Fair" watches often need service ($500-1000+ for luxury brands) plus possible parts replacement. Only buy if you're getting a deal accounting for restoration costs, or if you want a watch for parts.

6. Poor / Project Watch

Definition: Severely damaged, not running, or incomplete. For restoration or parts only.

Price discount: 60-80% below mint

What to expect:

  • Significant damage affecting functionality
  • Watch may not be running
  • Water damage or corrosion possible
  • Missing critical components
  • Crystal cracked or missing
  • Major dents, cracks, or structural damage

For collectors only: Unless you're a watchmaker or serious collector sourcing rare parts, avoid "Poor" condition. Restoration costs often exceed the watch's value.

Additional Condition Terms Decoded

Full Set vs. Watch Only

Separate from condition, documentation affects value significantly:

  • Full Set: Watch, original box, papers, warranty card, manuals, hang tags (+20-30% value)
  • Box & Papers: Watch, box, and papers only (+15-20% value)
  • Papers Only: Watch with warranty card/papers, no box (+10-15% value)
  • Watch Only: Just the watch, no documentation (baseline pricing)

Rolex, Omega, and other luxury brands see the highest premiums for full sets. Fashion watches see minimal impact.

Polished vs. Unpolished

Many collectors prefer unpolished watches that maintain original case proportions:

  • Unpolished: Original factory finishing preserved, may show wear (+5-10% for vintage)
  • Lightly polished: One or two professional services, no case shape distortion (neutral)
  • Over-polished: Multiple aggressive polishes, visible case thinning, rounded edges (-10-20%)

For modern sports watches (2000+), light polishing is acceptable and expected. For vintage pieces (pre-1990), unpolished is highly prized.

Original vs. Service Parts

Component originality matters tremendously:

  • 100% original: All components as delivered from factory (premium)
  • Service parts: Official replacement parts during service (slight discount on vintage)
  • Aftermarket parts: Non-OEM components (significant discount, avoid for investment pieces)
  • Refinished dial: Non-original dial refinishing (major red flag, -30-50% on vintage)

How to Evaluate Condition from Photos

When buying online, sharp photography skills are essential:

Red Flags in Listing Photos

  • Poor lighting or blurry images: Seller hiding condition issues
  • No close-ups of case, clasp, or crystal: Major wear being concealed
  • Stock photos only: Not the actual watch for sale
  • Odd angles avoiding certain areas: Damage on hidden side
  • Heavy filters or editing: Scratches being hidden digitally

What to Request from Sellers

  • Photos in natural daylight (reveals scratches)
  • Close-ups of case sides, lugs, and bracelet clasp
  • Crystal photo at an angle (shows scratches)
  • Bracelet stretch test (gap between links)
  • Video of watch running (confirms function)
  • Photos next to ruler or reference object (confirms authenticity)

Condition vs. Value: Making Smart Tradeoffs

Not everyone needs "Mint." Here's how to choose:

Buy Mint/LNIB if:

  • Planning to resell soon (condition premium recoups on exit)
  • Buying as investment or collection piece
  • Want maximum enjoyment from pristine appearance
  • Price difference is negligible (under 10%)

Buy Excellent if:

  • Best value for most buyers - looks great, saves money
  • Planning to wear daily (you'll add wear anyway)
  • Condition issues are minor and easily remedied (polish)
  • 15%+ discount from Mint pricing

Buy Good if:

  • Budget-conscious but want authentic luxury
  • Planning professional restoration anyway
  • Comfortable with visible wear patina
  • 25%+ discount from Mint pricing

Avoid Fair/Poor unless:

  • You're a watchmaker or restorer
  • Buying for parts on rare model
  • Total cost including service is still below market

Real-World Examples: Same Watch, Different Grades

Let's examine how condition affects pricing on a Rolex Submariner 116610LN:

  • NOS (2023): $10,500 - Stickers intact, unworn, full set
  • Mint (2022): $9,800 - Worn twice, flawless, full set
  • Excellent (2020): $8,900 - Light wear, minor scratches, full set
  • Good (2018): $7,500 - Visible wear throughout, watch only
  • Fair (2015): $6,200 - Heavy wear, needs service, watch only

Savings potential: $4,300 (41%) from NOS to Fair

The smart buyer? Someone who pays $8,900 for "Excellent," gets it professionally polished for $150, and has a watch indistinguishable from the $10,500 NOS version.

Condition Grading Across Brands

Some brands hold value better across condition grades:

  • Rolex/Patek: Condition premiums highest (30-40% spread Mint to Good)
  • Omega/Tudor: Moderate impact (20-30% spread)
  • Seiko/Hamilton: Minimal impact (10-15% spread)
  • Fashion watches: Condition almost irrelevant

The more expensive and collectible the brand, the more condition matters.

Your Action Plan

  1. Determine your priorities: Investment vs. daily wear vs. budget
  2. Set realistic condition expectations based on your use case
  3. Research market pricing at each condition level for your target watch
  4. Calculate restoration costs if buying below Excellent grade
  5. Demand detailed photos and video from any seller
  6. Use condition to negotiate - sellers often grade optimistically
  7. Buy from reputable dealers who provide accurate grading and returns

Final Thoughts

Understanding watch condition grading empowers you to make informed decisions and avoid overpaying. The "perfect" condition depends entirely on your needs—not everyone requires Mint.

For most buyers, Excellent condition offers the best value. You save 10-20% compared to Mint while getting a watch that looks fantastic on the wrist. Reserve Mint/LNIB purchases for investment pieces or when you plan to resell soon.
